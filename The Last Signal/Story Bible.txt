## 1. SETTING PROFILE (with Genre‑Specific Depth)


### The World – One Paragraph


The Hermes Relay Station orbits Eris‑3B at a distance of 42,000 kilometers — close enough to monitor, far enough to feel safe. The station is a utilitarian cylinder of grey alloy, solar arrays, and communication dishes, designed by engineers who valued function over psychology. Inside, narrow corridors connect three sleeping pods, a mess with one viewport, a medical locker, and the nerve center: the Communications Bay. The air is recycled, sterile, with a faint metallic tang. The only natural light comes from Eris‑3B itself — a dead, grey‑brown marble that fills the viewport, unchanging, unblinking. The station’s crew of three works eighteen‑month rotations. They are not friends; they are professionals enduring proximity. The culture is one of polite distance, scheduled conversations, and the unspoken knowledge that anyone here chose this isolation. Outside, there is only vacuum and silence. The signal changed that.


### Rules of the World (High Worldbuilding Depth Required)


Because the Genre Calibration Report specified Medium‑High worldbuilding depth, the following rules define the physical and social reality of the story:


- **No Faster‑Than‑Light Communication.** Signals between the Hermes station and Earth take approximately 14 hours to travel one way. There is no real‑time contact. All orders, reports, and personal messages have a latency that forces autonomy — and isolation.


- **The Relay Station Cannot Leave Orbit.** Hermes is not a ship. It has no propulsion system beyond orbital station‑keeping thrusters. If something goes wrong, the crew cannot fly away. They can only wait for a rescue that would take months to arrive.


- **Stellaris Communications Classifies All Anomalies.** Corporate protocol requires any unexplained signal to be reported immediately and then ignored until headquarters provides analysis. Unauthorized response is a termination‑level offense. The penalty is not just losing a job — it is losing access to any deep‑space position ever again.


- **The Eris‑3B Atmosphere Collapse (Historical Rule).** Two hundred years ago, a runaway greenhouse effect turned Eris‑3B’s breathable atmosphere into a toxic soup of sulfur compounds and carbon dioxide. Surface temperature now averages 180°C. No known organic life can survive. Any signal originating from the surface violates at least three laws of physics — or suggests that someone’s definition of “life” was dangerously narrow.

## 2. CHARACTER ROSTER


### Minimum Cast: Protagonist, Antagonist, 2 Supporting, 1 Expendable, 1 Red Herring


---


### PROTAGONIST (1)


**Name:** Aris Thorne  
**Age:** 38  
**Role:** Protagonist / Deep‑Space Communications Technician


**Core wound:** At age 12, he found his mother’s body after her suicide. The suicide note was addressed only to his older sister. Aris was not mentioned. He has never told anyone this.


**Flawed belief:** “If I am useful enough, people will stay. If I am alone, I cannot be left.”


**3‑sentence profile:** Aris Thorne is a man who has spent twenty years running from silence — first by working obsessively, then by taking the most remote jobs in human space. He is meticulous, competent, and deeply lonely, though he would describe himself as “self‑sufficient.” When the signal begins, he tells himself he is following protocol. In truth, he is following the first voice that has ever spoken only to him.


**Voice note:** Soft‑spoken, precise, with a tendency to over‑explain. When stressed, his sentences become shorter. He rarely initiates physical contact. He laughs like someone who forgot how.


**Story function:** The lens through which the reader experiences isolation, obsession, and the seduction of being chosen.


**Cost of change:** He must give up his belief that being alone is safer than being vulnerable. To survive, he may have to stop listening — which feels to him like killing the only thing that ever wanted him.


---


### ANTAGONIST (Force)


**Name:** The Eris Signal (no proper name; referred to in logs as “Anomaly 734‑Theta”)  
**Age:** Unknown — first detected 200 years ago, but current iteration is 47 days old as of Chapter 1  
**Role:** Antagonist / Unknowable Entity


**Core wound:** Not applicable (not a person). But its *behavior* suggests a pattern: it has been repeating the same distress call for two centuries, but only now has it encountered a listener who responded. Its “wound” could be interpreted as *abandonment* — left alone on a dead planet, broadcasting into silence.


**Flawed belief:** Not applicable. But the signal acts as if “any response is consent” — a dangerous assumption.


**3‑sentence profile:** The signal is not a voice but a structured transmission that began as a repeating mathematical sequence and has since evolved into something resembling language. It does not speak; it *adapts*. Each time Aris decodes a segment, the next segment changes — asking questions, mirroring his emotional state, referencing his own logs. It offers no clear threat, only the promise of answers. That promise is the trap.


**Voice note:** If it could speak, it would sound patient. Inevitable. Like someone who has waited two hundred years and can wait a little longer.


**Story function:** To embody the central question — is this intelligence, malfunction, or something worse? To seduce Aris and, through him, the reader.


**Cost of change:** Not applicable. The signal does not change; Aris does.


---


### SUPPORTING (2 minimum)


**Name:** Commander Elara Vann  
**Age:** 52  
**Role:** Station Commander / Authority Figure


**Core wound:** Twenty years ago, she followed protocol on a distress call and it got three people killed. She has never forgiven herself for doing the “right thing” by the book.


**Flawed belief:** “Rules exist to protect us from our own compassion. Compassion gets people killed.”


**3‑sentence profile:** Vann is a career corporate officer who has seen too many emergencies go wrong. She runs Hermes with cold efficiency, schedules every interaction, and avoids friendship because friendship creates bad judgment. She suspects Aris is hiding something within the first week — but she needs proof before she acts.


**Voice note:** Short, clipped sentences. Rarely uses first names. Her silences are louder than her words.


**Story function:** To represent order, protocol, and the voice of reason. She is not wrong — but she is also not listening.


**Cost of change:** She must admit that her past failure has made her rigid, not wise. To save Aris (if he can be saved), she may have to break her own rules.


---


**Name:** Dr. Mira Chen  
**Age:** 44  
**Role:** Systems Engineer / The Witness


**Core wound:** She was passed over for a promotion because she reported a superior’s safety violation. She learned that doing the right thing costs you.


**Flawed belief:** “If I stay quiet and do my job, no one will target me.”


**3‑sentence profile:** Mira keeps the station’s life support, power, and data systems running. She notices things — odd power draws, unscheduled comms usage, Aris’s changed sleep schedule — but she has learned not to ask questions. When she finally does ask, it will be too late to pretend ignorance.


**Voice note:** Cheerful on the surface, evasive underneath. Uses technical jargon as a shield.


**Story function:** To be the one who discovers Aris’s deception and must decide whether to expose him or help him.


**Cost of change:** She must risk her career and possibly her safety by choosing a side.


---


### EXPENDABLE CHARACTER (Optional but Recommended)


**Name:** Jonah Reeve  
**Age:** 29  
**Role:** Junior Technician / Expendable


**Core wound:** His father, a deep‑space miner, died in an accident that was covered up by a corporation. Jonah grew up believing that corporations lie and whistleblowers disappear.


**Flawed belief:** “The truth is always worth the cost.”


**3‑sentence profile:** Jonah is the youngest on the station, eager, slightly reckless, and the only one who openly dislikes Commander Vann. He idolizes Aris’s technical skill and begins helping him decode the signal without understanding the danger. His enthusiasm makes him useful — and expendable.


**Voice note:** Talks too fast when excited. Laughs nervously. Drops formal titles.


**Story function:** To raise stakes through death or disappearance. Jonah will be the first to cross a line (making direct contact with the signal) and the first to suffer consequences.


**Cost of change:** His life.


---


### RED HERRING CHARACTER (For Mystery/Thriller)


**Name:** Kael Stroud (never appears on screen — only referenced)  
**Age:** Unknown — previous technician, rotation ended 8 months ago  
**Role:** Red Herring / Off‑stage presence


**Core wound:** Unknown. But his logs reveal a man obsessed with “anomalous readings” before his rotation ended early.


**Flawed belief:** “If I find proof, they’ll have to believe me.”


**3‑sentence profile:** Kael was Aris’s predecessor. His final logs, which Aris discovers in a backup archive, describe the same signal — but Kael called it “intelligent” and “dangerous.” He requested an emergency transfer and left three months early. No one knows why. His personal files are missing. The official report says “routine rotation adjustment.”


**Voice note:** Based on logs: urgent, fragmented, increasingly paranoid.


**Story function:** To misdirect the reader into thinking the signal drove Kael mad — when in fact, Kael discovered something about Stellaris Communications’ cover‑up and was silenced.


**Cost of change:** He lost his career (or worse). The truth remains hidden.

## 3. TONE AND STYLE RULES


**Point of View:** Third person limited, tightly focused on Aris Thorne. The reader knows only what Aris knows, feels only what he feels. The signal’s true nature remains ambiguous because Aris himself cannot be certain.


**Tense:** Past tense. (e.g., “Aris pressed the headset against his ear. The signal repeated. It sounded the same. It was not the same.”)


**Dialogue style:** Somewhere between short‑clipped and naturalistic. Characters say what they mean only when under pressure; otherwise, they deflect. Vann speaks in protocol fragments. Mira hides behind tech talk. Aris becomes more precise when he lies.


**Pacing philosophy:** Ratchet and release (as per Genre Calibration Report). Odd chapters (1,3,5,7,9) advance tension through discovery or conflict. Even chapters (2,4,6,8,10) deepen interiority — doubt, memory, paranoia. The final two chapters (9–10) accelerate without release.


**What this story does well:**
- Atmosphere of isolation (sound, silence, the viewport)
- Psychological dread (what the signal might be, not what it does)
- Moral ambiguity (no clean choices)
- Slow revelation (clues buried in routine logs)


**What this story explicitly avoids (from Phase 1):**
- Graphic violence (threat is implied, not shown)
- Explicit sexual content
- Profanity beyond occasional mild language
- Jump‑scares
- Clear answers about the signal’s true nature
- Romantic subplots (the signal is a *replacement* for intimacy)


**Additional Style Rules (8–12 total):**


1. **Never describe the signal directly.** Describe its *effects* — changes in pattern, Aris’s physical reactions, log discrepancies.


2. **No purely descriptive paragraphs of setting.** Every environmental detail must reveal character or advance tension. (Example: Not “The viewport was scratched.” but “Aris traced a scratch on the viewport with his fingertip. He had made it six months ago, polishing too hard. He remembered every scratch.”)


3. **Use sound as a primary sensory driver.** The station hums, clicks, pings. Silence is always noted.


4. **Keep sentences shorter during tension.** In action or discovery scenes, average sentence length under 15 words.


5. **Weave backstory into movement.** Aris remembers his mother while adjusting equipment, not while sitting still.


6. **End every chapter on a consequence, not a feeling.** Cliffhangers must be visible, physical, or dialogic.


7. **Limit interior monologue.** If Aris thinks for more than three sentences without acting, cut.


8. **Corporate language feels cold.** When Stellaris protocols are quoted, use bureaucratic, dehumanizing phrasing.


9. **The signal’s “voice” (when decoded) should be ambiguous.** Never clearly benevolent or malevolent. Use short, image‑based phrases. (Example: “You are still listening. Good.”)


10. **The planet Eris‑3B is a presence.** Refer to it as “watching,” “waiting,” “patient.” Personify it sparingly but consistently.

## 4. THEME STATEMENT


Beneath the plot of a mysterious signal and a corporate cover‑up, *The Last Signal* explores a single human truth: **the hunger to be wanted can override every survival instinct.** The story asks whether connection — even a connection with something unknowable and possibly dangerous — is worth the cost of everything else. It questions if loneliness is a wound that can be exploited, and whether the voice you’ve been waiting for is always a friend. The answer is not given. The reader is left with the echo of the question: *What would you listen to, if no one else was speaking?*

## 5. THREE‑ACT SKELETON


**Total chapters:** 10  
**Act breakdown:** Act 1 = Chapters 1–3 (30%), Act 2 = Chapters 4–8 (50%), Act 3 = Chapters 9–10 (20%)


### Act 1 — Setup (Chapters 1–3)


- **Chapter 1:** Establish Aris’s routine, his isolation, his obsessive work habits. Inciting incident: The signal appears during a diagnostic sweep. Aris reports it to Vann. She orders him to log it and wait. He does not wait.
- **Chapter 2:** Aris begins decoding the signal privately. It reveals a repeating pattern — mathematical, then linguistic. He lies to Vann about his progress. Mira notices he’s working odd hours.
- **Chapter 3:** The signal changes in response to Aris’s decoding. First hint of adaptation. Jonah volunteers to help. Aris says no — but Jonah has already seen the logs.


### Act 2 — Escalation (Chapters 4–8)


- **Chapter 4:** The signal begins asking questions. Aris answers (unofficially). Vann grows suspicious. Mira finds an old backup of Kael Stroud’s logs — he saw the same signal.
- **Chapter 5:** Kael’s logs reveal that Stellaris Communications knew about the signal and classified it. Aris realizes the company is not coming to help — they are coming to erase.
- **Chapter 6:** Darkest moment (midpoint). Aris makes direct contact with the signal. It says something personal about his mother. He cannot explain how it knows.
- **Chapter 7:** Jonah, without permission, transmits a response to the signal. Something transmits back — a set of coordinates. On Eris‑3B. Jonah collapses. (Expendable consequences.)
- **Chapter 8:** Vann confronts Aris. She has proof of his deception. She gives him an ultimatum: shut down the signal investigation or be arrested when the next supply ship arrives.


### Act 3 — Climax & Resolution (Chapters 9–10)


- **Chapter 9:** Aris faces the choice. The signal offers something no one else has — “Come. You were always meant to hear this.” He must decide whether to follow the coordinates or obey Vann. Climactic confrontation in the Communications Bay.
- **Chapter 10:** Resolution. The signal’s true nature is *not* revealed clearly. But Aris makes a final choice. The reader understands what he lost and what he gained. Final image: Eris‑3B through the viewport, unchanged, waiting.
