The Story Engine
A Repeatable Framework for Building Any Multi-Chapter Story with an LLM
________________




HOW THIS WORKS
This framework runs in 4 Phases. Each phase produces a document you keep. By the end of Phase 4, you have everything you need to write every chapter.
PHASE 1 — Story DNA        (10 questions → your core concept)
PHASE 2 — Story Bible      (characters, world, tone, rules)
PHASE 3 — Chapter Blueprint (chapter-by-chapter outline with cliffhangers)
PHASE 4 — Writing Prompts  (scene-by-scene prompts for your LLM)




You can run all four phases in one session or spread them across multiple sessions. Every phase produces output you save before moving to the next one.

________________




PHASE 1 — STORY DNA
Paste this into your LLM first. Answer the questions. Save the output.
________________




You are a story development consultant helping me design a multi-chapter novel or novella from scratch.




Ask me the following 10 questions, one at a time. Wait for my answer before asking the next one. After I answer all 10, produce a STORY DNA SUMMARY in the format shown below.




THE 10 QUESTIONS:




1. GENRE: What genre or genre combination interests you?
   (Examples: mystery/thriller, romance, historical fiction, science fiction, fantasy, horror,
   adventure, literary fiction, western, spy thriller — or a combination like
   "mystery with romance" or "sci-fi with family drama")




2. SETTING: What kind of world or environment feels right?
   (Examples: modern city, small town, historical period, space, alternate reality,
   near future, remote wilderness, foreign country — be as specific or vague as you like)




3. TIME PERIOD: When is this story set?
   (Past / present / future — if past or future, roughly when?)




4. CENTRAL CONFLICT: What is the engine of the story?
   (Examples: a crime to solve, a secret to uncover, a journey to complete,
   a relationship to save, a war to survive, an identity to discover)




5. EMOTIONAL CORE: What feeling do you want readers to carry when it's over?
   (Examples: hope, grief, justice, wonder, love, fear, redemption,
   the world is more complex than I thought)




6. PROTAGONIST: Give me the protagonist's **core wound** (a past trauma that shapes their present), their **self-sabotaging habit** (how they avoid healing), and their **hidden desire** (what they secretly want vs. what they claim to want).
   (Example: 'Core wound: Abandoned by mother at 8. Self-sabotaging habit: Overworks to avoid intimacy. Hidden desire: To be chosen unconditionally, but claims to want "success."')




7. ANTAGONIST OR OBSTACLE: What stands in the protagonist's way?
   (Can be a person, a system, a force of nature, the protagonist's own psychology,
   or a combination)




8. SECONDARY THREADS: Beyond the central conflict, what other threads do you want?
   (Examples: a love story, a friendship tested, a family secret, a moral dilemma,
   a coming-of-age arc — pick 1 or 2)




9. TONE AND CONTENT RULES: What do you want to avoid?
   (Examples: graphic violence, explicit romance, profanity, dark themes,
   political content, religious content — be honest, this shapes everything)




10. LENGTH: How long do you want the story?
    (Short: 5–7 chapters / Medium: 8–12 chapters / Long: 13–20 chapters)

PHASE 2 — STORY BIBLE
Paste your Phase 1 summary + this prompt. Save the output.
________________




Here is my Story DNA Summary:




[PASTE YOUR PHASE 1 OUTPUT HERE]




Using this summary, build a complete STORY BIBLE. The story bible is the document I will paste at the start of every writing session to keep my LLM focused. It should include:




1. SETTING PROFILE
Write one full paragraph describing the story's world, location, and atmosphere. Include:
- Physical details (geography, architecture, climate, time period visuals)
- Social details (who lives here, what tensions exist, what is the culture)
- The "feel" of the setting (what emotion does this place produce in the reader)




2. CHARACTER ROSTER
For each character, provide:
- Name and age
- Role (protagonist / antagonist / supporting / expendable / red herring)
- **Core wound:** How a past trauma manifests in *current behavior* (e.g., ‘Flies into rage when criticized → abandoned as a child’).
- **Flawed belief:** A lie they tell themselves (e.g., ‘If I’m perfect, people will stay’).
- 3-sentence profile: who they are, what they want (**externally**), what they hide
- Voice note: one sentence describing how they speak and carry themselves
- Story function: what narrative job does this character perform?
- **Cost of change:** What must they sacrifice or lose to grow? (e.g., 'His sense of total control').




Include at minimum:
  - 1–2 protagonists
  - 1 antagonist (person, institution, or force)
  - 2–3 supporting characters
  - 1 expendable character (someone whose death or loss raises the stakes — optional but recommended)
  - 1 red herring character (someone the reader suspects incorrectly — optional but recommended for mystery/thriller)


**Flag any character lacking a specific core wound or flawed belief. These are mandatory for protagonists and major supporting cast.**




3. TONE AND STYLE RULES
A list of 8–12 rules for how this story should be written. Include:
- Point of view (first person / third person limited / third person omniscient)
- Tense (past / present)
- Dialogue style (long and flowing / short and clipped / somewhere between)
- Pacing philosophy (slow burn / fast / varied)
- What this story does well (atmosphere, tension, character interiority, action, etc.)
- What this story explicitly avoids (based on the content rules from Phase 1)




4. THEME STATEMENT
One paragraph on what this story is really about beneath the plot.
What human truth does it explore? What question does it ask that it may or may not answer?




5. THREE-ACT SKELETON
A high-level structural map:
- Act 1 (chapters 1 to ~25% of total): Setup, inciting incident, world established
- Act 2 (chapters ~25%–75%): Escalation, complications, reversals, darkest moment
- Act 3 (chapters ~75%–end): Climax, resolution, final emotional beat




Save this story bible before moving to Phase 3.

---




After all chapters are outlined, produce:




STORY STRUCTURE MAP
A summary table showing how each chapter serves the three acts, which threads are active, and when the major turning points occur.




CLIFFHANGER AUDIT
Review all cliffhangers. Flag any that:
- Repeat the same type (e.g., three "mysterious text message" cliffhangers in a row)
- Land too weakly (hint without specificity)
- Give away too much too early
Suggest replacements for any flagged cliffhangers.


**DEPTH AUDIT**
**Review Chapters 1-3 and flag the following:**
- **Any scene where the protagonist is a passive observer.**
- **Any "Character Beat" that is passive (e.g., "feels sad") instead of active (e.g., "destroys evidence").**
- **Any cliffhanger that is a random external event instead of a consequence of a character's flaw.**
**Suggest concrete replacements for any flagged items.**

Using both documents, build a complete WRITING PROMPT PACKAGE. This is the document I will use during actual chapter writing with my LLM.




The package should contain, in this order:




---




SECTION 1: MASTER SYSTEM PROMPT
A single block of text (clearly marked) that I paste at the start of every writing session.
It should include:
- Story title and genre
- One-paragraph story summary
- Complete character list with voice notes
- All tone and style rules
- POV and tense reminder
- Chapter length targets
- The content rules (what to avoid)
**- GOLDEN RULE: SHOW, DON'T TELL. Never state a character's emotion directly (e.g., 'he was sad'). Instead, reveal it through physical sensation (e.g., 'a weight settled on his chest'), action (e.g., 'he stared at the phone, unable to dial'), or dialogue subtext. Weave character backstory and motivation into the narrative through subtle memories triggered by the environment.**
This block should be self-contained — everything the LLM needs to stay on track without reading any other document.




---




SECTION 2: CHAPTER LENGTH GUIDE
A reference table showing:
- Words per chapter (range and target)
- Paragraphs per chapter (range)
- Scenes per chapter (range)
- Dialogue exchanges per chapter (range)
- Cliffhanger length (in sentences)
- Note极速加速器 how to handle longer or shorter chapters




---




SECTION 3: INDIVIDUAL CHAPTER PROMPTS
For every chapter, one prompt block (clearly marked CHAPTER [N]) containing:




CHAPTER CONTEXT (brief — 2–3 sentences orienting the LLM to where we are in the story)




Then, for each scene in the chapter:




SCENE [N] PROMPT — a complete, specific prompt block the LLM can act on directly. Each scene prompt must include:
- Location and time of day
- Who is present
- What happens (the plot mechanics)
- **What **flawed action** the POV character takes (e.g., lying, avoiding a question, choosing isolation).**
- What the scene must accomplish (its narrative job)
- Any specific character beats, dialogue moments, or sensory details to include
- **Sensory details that reflect the POV character's emotional state (e.g., "The rain sounded like a ticking clock" to show anxiety).**
- The emotional state that should carry through the scene
- How the scene ends (transition line or setup for next scene)
- **A ban on purely descriptive adjectives for setting. Description must serve character revelation.**




End each chapter prompt with:
CLIFFHANGER INSTRUCTION — exact guidance on how to write the chapter's final beat, including what the reader should and should not understand at this moment. **This cliffhanger must be a direct consequence of a character's flawed choice from earlier in the chapter.**




---




SECTION 4: UTILITY PROMPTS
Four reusable helper prompts, written specifically for this story:




1. CONTINUATION PROMPT — for when a scene comes out too short
2. RESET ANCHOR — for when the LLM loses the tone or character voice (include 3 specific reminders based on this story's rules)
3. SCENE TRANSITION PROMPT — for moving cleanly between scenes
4. CLIFFHANGER SHARPENER — for tightening a weak chapter ending




---




At the top of the complete document, include:
- Story title
- Genre
- Total chapter count
- Estimated total word count
- A one-line note on the story's emotional core
