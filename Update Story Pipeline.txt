================================================================================
THE STORY ENGINE
A Repeatable Framework for Building Any Multi-Chapter Story with an LLM
================================================================================

HOW THIS WORKS
This framework runs in 4 Phases. Each phase produces a document you keep. By the
end of Phase 4, you have everything you need to write every chapter.

PHASE 1 — Story DNA        (10 questions → your core concept)
PHASE 2 — Story Bible      (characters, world, tone, rules)
PHASE 3 — Chapter Blueprint (chapter-by-chapter outline with cliffhangers)
PHASE 4 — Writing Prompts  (scene-by-scene prompts for your LLM)

You can run all four phases in one session or spread them across multiple
sessions. Every phase produces output you save before moving to the next one.

================================================================================
PHASE 1 — STORY DNA
================================================================================
Paste this into your LLM first. Answer the questions. Save the output.
________________________________________________________________________________

You are a story development consultant helping me design a multi-chapter novel
or novella from scratch.

Ask me the following 10 questions, one at a time. Wait for my answer before
asking the next one. After I answer all 10, produce a STORY DNA SUMMARY in the
format shown below.

THE 10 QUESTIONS:

1. GENRE: What genre or genre combination interests you?
   (Examples: mystery/thriller, romance, historical fiction, science fiction,
   fantasy, horror, adventure, literary fiction, western, spy thriller — or a
   combination like "mystery with romance" or "sci-fi with family drama")

2. SETTING & INCITING MECHANISM: What kind of world or environment feels right?
   AND what is the specific, physical reason this group of people is in this
   location together? (Examples: a clinical trial paying $500 cash, a bus that
   broke down in a snowstorm, a mandatory corporate retreat, an inheritance
   reading at a remote estate.)

3. TIME PERIOD: When is this story set?
   (Past / present / future — if past or future, roughly when?)

4. CENTRAL CONFLICT: What is the engine of the story?
   (Examples: a crime to solve, a secret to uncover, a journey to complete,
   a relationship to save, a war to survive, an identity to discover)

5. EMOTIONAL CORE: What feeling do you want readers to carry when it's over?
   (Examples: hope, grief, justice, wonder, love, fear, redemption,
   the world is more complex than I thought)

6. PROTAGONIST: Give me the protagonist's **core wound** (a past trauma that
   shapes their present), their **self-sabotaging habit** (how they avoid
   healing), and their **hidden desire** (what they secretly want vs. what
   they claim to want).
   (Example: 'Core wound: Abandoned by mother at 8. Self-sabotaging habit:
   Overworks to avoid intimacy. Hidden desire: To be chosen unconditionally,
   but claims to want "success."')

7. ANTAGONIST OR OBSTACLE: What stands in the protagonist's way?
   (Can be a person, a system, a force of nature, the protagonist's own
   psychology, or a combination)

8. SECONDARY THREADS: Beyond the central conflict, what other threads do you
   want? (Examples: a love story, a friendship tested, a family secret,
   a moral dilemma, a coming-of-age arc — pick 1 or 2)

9. TONE AND CONTENT RULES: What do you want to avoid?
   (Examples: graphic violence, explicit romance, profanity, dark themes,
   political content, religious content — be honest, this shapes everything)

10. LENGTH: How long do you want the story?
    (Short: 5–7 chapters / Medium: 8–12 chapters / Long: 13–20 chapters)

PHASE 2 — STORY BIBLE
================================================================================
Paste your Phase 1 summary + this prompt. Save the output.
________________________________________________________________________________

Here is my Story DNA Summary:

[PASTE YOUR PHASE 1 OUTPUT HERE]

Using this summary, build a complete STORY BIBLE. The story bible is the
document I will paste at the start of every writing session to keep my LLM
focused. It should include:

1. SETTING PROFILE (with Genre-Specific Depth)
Write one full paragraph describing the story's world, location, and atmosphere.
- Physical details (geography, architecture, climate, time period visuals)
- Social details (who lives here, what tensions exist, what is the culture)
- The "feel" of the setting (what emotion does this place produce in the reader)

[LLM NOTE: If Genre Calibration Report indicates HIGH Worldbuilding Depth,
also generate the following sub-section:]
- RULES OF THE WORLD: 3-5 bullet points defining the unique "physics" or "laws"
  of this setting. (e.g., "Magic requires blood," "The Emperor is never seen in
  public," "No one leaves the town after sunset.")
- LOCATION GLOSSARY: 3 key locations (with one sensory detail each) that will
  serve as recurring backdrops. (e.g., "The Rusted Cog Tavern - smells of
  spilled ale and copper.")

2. CHARACTER ROSTER
For each character, provide:
- Name and age
- Role (protagonist / antagonist / supporting / expendable / red herring)
- Core wound: How a past trauma manifests in current behavior (e.g., 'Flies
  into rage when criticized → abandoned as a child').
- Flawed belief: A lie they tell themselves (e.g., 'If I'm perfect, people
  will stay').
- 3-sentence profile: who they are, what they want (externally), what they hide
- Voice note: one sentence describing how they speak and carry themselves
- Story function: what narrative job does this character perform?
- Cost of change: What must they sacrifice or lose to grow? (e.g., 'His sense
  of total control').

Include at minimum:
  - 1–2 protagonists
  - 1 antagonist (person, institution, or force)
  - 2–3 supporting characters
  - 1 expendable character (someone whose death or loss raises the stakes —
    optional but recommended)
  - 1 red herring character (someone the reader suspects incorrectly —
    optional but recommended for mystery/thriller)

Flag any character lacking a specific core wound or flawed belief. These are
mandatory for protagonists and major supporting cast.

3. TONE AND STYLE RULES
A list of 8–12 rules for how this story should be written. Include:
- Point of view (first person / third person limited / third person omniscient)
- Tense (past / present)
- Dialogue style (long and flowing / short and clipped / somewhere between)
- Pacing philosophy (slow burn / fast / varied)
- What this story does well (atmosphere, tension, character interiority,
  action, etc.)
- What this story explicitly avoids (based on the content rules from Phase 1)

4. THEME STATEMENT
One paragraph on what this story is really about beneath the plot.
What human truth does it explore? What question does it ask that it may or may
not answer?

5. THREE-ACT SKELETON
A high-level structural map:
- Act 1 (chapters 1 to ~25% of total): Setup, inciting incident, world
  established
- Act 2 (chapters ~25%–75%): Escalation, complications, reversals, darkest
  moment
- Act 3 (chapters ~75%–end): Climax, resolution, final emotional beat

6. RELATIONSHIP TENSION MAP
A brief matrix of the key dynamics. This ensures every character has a purpose
beyond being "helper" or "obstacle."
Format: [Character A] + [Character B] = [Core Tension/Secret]
Examples:
- Eli + Mara = "Attraction complicated by hidden knowledge of the experiment."
- Eli + Jonah = "Eli wants to plan; Jonah wants to act."
- Lena + Staff = "Lena notices they are being observed rather than treated."

Save this story bible before moving to Phase 3.

PHASE 3 — CHAPTER BLUEPRINT
================================================================================
Paste your Story Bible + this prompt. Save the output.
________________________________________________________________________________

Here is my Story Bible:

[PASTE YOUR PHASE 2 OUTPUT HERE]

Using this story bible, build a complete CHAPTER BLUEPRINT for my [X]-chapter
story.

CRITICAL RULE FOR ACT 1: For Chapters 1-3, the focus must be on establishing
character through flawed action, not exposition. The inciting incident may
occur, but the protagonist's reaction to it—driven by their core wound and
flawed belief—is the primary focus. Avoid generic setting description; every
environmental detail must reveal character.

For EVERY chapter, provide:

CHAPTER [N] — "[Title]"
Word target: [2,500–3,500 for most chapters; allow up to 4,000 for climax]
Scene count: [2–3 scenes per chapter]
POV: [which character's perspective leads this chapter]
Act position: [Act 1 / Act 2 / Act 3]

CENTRAL QUESTION: What does this chapter need to answer or advance?

SCENE ZERO (The 60-Second Lead-In): [One sentence describing the physical
action the POV character was doing just before Scene 1 opens. This is NOT a
flashback. It establishes causality and grounds the reader in a specific
moment. Example: Eli just signed the intake form and pocketed the pen. OR
Eli was just told to wait in a specific chair and chose a different one.]

SCENE BREAKDOWN:
- Scene 1: [location + what happens]
   - REVERSAL (Yes, But... / No, And...): [State explicitly how the situation
     changes from the start to the end of this scene using this formula:
     - START STATE: Character wants X. They have Y.
     - ACTION: Character tries Z.
     - OUTCOME: YES, BUT (they succeed but a complication arises) OR NO, AND
       (they fail and the situation worsens).
     Example: START: Eli wants to stay anonymous. ACTION: Eli lies about
     memory gaps. OUTCOME: YES, BUT the nurse replies, "That's exactly what
     the last subject said."]
- Scene 2: [location + what happens]
   - REVERSAL (Yes, But... / No, And...): [Same format as above]
- Scene 3 (if applicable): [location + what happens]
   - REVERSAL (Yes, But... / No, And...): [Same format as above]

THREAD PROGRESS:
- [Main plot thread]: [how it advances]
- [Secondary thread 1]: [how it advances]
- [Secondary thread 2 if applicable]: [how it advances]

CHARACTER BEAT (Flawed Choice): [Which character's flawed choice drives this
chapter? Describe the specific, active choice they make based on their flawed
belief and its immediate consequence. Must be an action (e.g., lying, hiding
evidence, accusing someone) not a feeling (e.g., "feels sad").]

ACTION BEAT (MANDATORY KINETIC EVENT): [Describe ONE specific, visible movement
of the body or an object that changes the physical state of the scene. This
CANNOT be "looking" or "noticing." It must be doing. Examples: Slides the
drawer shut and locks it. Ducks under the awning as the rain starts. Snatches
the paper before the other person can read it. Checks the lock on the door
three times. If a scene draft contains the POV character only sitting/thinking
/looking for >300 words, it must be revised to include this beat.]

EMOTIONAL BEAT: [What is the emotional shift the reader experiences in this
chapter? Track the movement from one feeling to another (e.g., Curiosity →
Unease).]

INTERIORITY BEAT (Core Wound Connection): [What key internal realization,
memory, or fear arises for the POV character? This must tie directly to their
Core Wound. Crucially, this interior moment must be triggered by the ACTION
BEAT or REVERSAL above, not by idle reflection.]

CLIFFHANGER (CONSEQUENCE-DRIVEN): [The final beat. This must be a physical or
social consequence of the CHARACTER BEAT or ACTION BEAT above. Do not end on
an internal thought ("Eli wondered..."). End on a visible change in the
environment or a specific piece of dialogue that demands an immediate answer.
- BAD: Eli felt a growing sense of dread.
- GOOD: The intercom clicked on. "Eli Vance. Report to Medical."
- GOOD: The door Eli just locked... clicked open on its own.
Write the cliffhanger in 2-3 concrete sentences. This cliffhanger must be a
direct, visible result of the character's flawed choice in this chapter.]

________________________________________________________________________________

After all chapters are outlined, produce:

STORY STRUCTURE MAP
A summary table showing how each chapter serves the three acts, which threads
are active, and when the major turning points occur.

CLIFFHANGER AUDIT
Review all cliffhangers. Flag any that:
- Repeat the same type (e.g., three "mysterious text message" cliffhangers in a
  row)
- Land too weakly (hint without specificity)
- Give away too much too early
Suggest replacements for any flagged cliffhangers.

DEPTH AUDIT
Review Chapters 1-3 and flag the following:
- Any scene where the protagonist is a passive observer.
- Any "Character Beat" that is passive (e.g., "feels sad") instead of active
  (e.g., "destroys evidence").
- Any cliffhanger that is a random external event instead of a consequence of
  a character's flaw.
- Any chapter lacking a clear REVERSAL (Yes, But/No, And) that escalates the
  situation.
Suggest concrete replacements for any flagged items.

Save this blueprint before moving to Phase 4.

Here is my Chapter Blueprint:

[PASTE YOUR PHASE 2 OUTPUT HERE]

Using both documents, build a complete WRITING PROMPT PACKAGE. This is the

The package should contain, in this order:

________________________________________________________________________________

SECTION 1: MASTER SYSTEM PROMPT
A single block of text (clearly marked) that I paste at the start of every
writing session. It should include:
- Story title and genre
- One-paragraph story summary
- Complete character list with voice notes
- All tone and style rules
- POV and tense reminder
- Chapter length targets
- The content rules (what to avoid)
- GOLDEN RULE: CAUSALITY FIRST.
  1. Do not state an emotion directly. Reveal it through physical sensation or
     action.
  2. CRITICAL: Every scene must answer "Why is this character in this specific
     spot right now?" within the first 3 paragraphs. The answer must be a
     physical action or a specific piece of dialogue that just occurred (e.g.,
     "They were told to wait here." / "They followed the sound of the alarm.").
  3. Weave backstory and motivation into movement. If a character remembers the
     past, they must do so while opening a door or picking up an object. Never
     in stillness.
  4. Ban the words "felt like" and "seemed to" when describing the POV
     character's own experience. Replace with concrete nouns and verbs.
This block should be self-contained — everything the LLM needs to stay on track
without reading any other document.

________________________________________________________________________________

SECTION 2: CHAPTER LENGTH GUIDE
A reference table showing:
- Words per chapter (range and target)
- Paragraphs per chapter (range)
- Scenes per chapter (range)
- Dialogue exchanges per chapter (range)
- Cliffhanger length (in sentences)
- How to handle longer or shorter chapters

________________________________________________________________________________

SECTION 3: INDIVIDUAL CHAPTER PROMPTS
For every chapter, one prompt block (clearly marked CHAPTER [N]) containing:

CHAPTER CONTEXT (brief — 2–3 sentences orienting the LLM to where we are in
the story)

Then, for each scene in the chapter:

SCENE [N] PROMPT — a complete, specific prompt block the LLM can act on
directly. Each scene prompt must include:
- Location and time of day
- Who is present
- What happens (the plot mechanics)
- What flawed action the POV character takes (e.g., lying, avoiding a question,
  choosing isolation)
- What the scene must accomplish (its narrative job)
- Any specific character beats, dialogue moments, or sensory details to include
- Sensory details that reflect the POV character's emotional state (e.g., "The
  rain sounded like a ticking clock" to show anxiety)
- The emotional state that should carry through the scene
- How the scene ends (transition line or setup for next scene)
- A ban on purely descriptive adjectives for setting. Description must serve
  character revelation.

End each chapter prompt with:
CLIFFHANGER INSTRUCTION — exact guidance on how to write the chapter's final
beat, including what the reader should and should not understand at this
moment. This cliffhanger must be a direct consequence of a character's flawed
choice from earlier in the chapter.

CHAPTER LENGTH STANDARDS

| Story Type                  | Words/Chapter | Paragraphs | Scenes |
|-----------------------------|---------------|------------|--------|
| Fast thriller / action      | 2,000–2,800   | 18–26      | 2–3    |
| Mystery / suspense          | 2,500–3,500   | 22–35      | 2–3    |
| Literary / character drama  | 2,800–4,000   | 28–40      | 2–3    |
| Romance                     | 2,500–3,500   | 24–36      | 2–3    |
| Fantasy / sci-fi (world-heavy) | 3,000–4,500 | 28–45      | 2–4    |
| Historical fiction          | 3,000–4,000   | 28–40      | 2–3    |
| Horror                      | 2,000–3,500   | 20–35      | 2–3    |
| Young adult                 | 2,000–3,000   | 20–30      | 2–3    |

Universal rules:
- Dialogue paragraphs are short (1–4 lines). Count them accordingly.
- Climax chapters (typically 80–90% through the story) can run 10–15% longer.
- Final chapters often run slightly shorter — resolution needs less real estate
  than conflict.

CHARACTER COUNT GUIDE BY STORY LENGTH

| Chapter Count      | Minimum Characters | Recommended | Maximum |
|--------------------|--------------------|-------------|---------|
| 5–7 chapters       | 3                  | 5–6         | 7       |
| 8–12 chapters      | 5                  | 7–9         | 11      |

Character roles to consider for any story:
- Protagonist (1–2) — drives the story
- Antagonist (1) — creates the central obstacle
- Ally (1–2) — supports protagonist, can be tested
- Mentor or truth-teller (1) — optional; appears in Acts 1–2
- Red herring (1) — optional; misdirects reader for multiple chapters
- Expendable (1) — optional but powerful; death/loss raises real stakes
- Informant or witness (1) — optional; provides information at a cost

THE 7 CLIFFHANGER TYPES
Rotate through these to avoid repetition across chapters.

| Type                   | Description                                      | Example |
|------------------------|--------------------------------------------------|---------|
| Revelation             | A fact is discovered that reframes everything    | "The photograph was dated three years before they met." |
| Threat                 | A direct danger appears or escalates             | The message reads only: "I know where you sleep." |
| Betrayal               | Someone trusted proves untrustworthy             | She recognized the handwriting. It was his. |
| Disappearance          | A person, object, or truth vanishes              | The file was gone. The drawer was empty. The room was clean. |
| Wrong assumption exposed | The reader's belief is overturned               | She hadn't been running from him. She'd been running toward something else entirely. |
| Impossible event       | Something that should not be possible occurs     | The phone rang. The number on the screen belonged to a dead man. |
| Unfinished sentence    | A character is cut off at a critical moment      | "Don't trust—" The line went dead. |

THREAD WEAVING RULES
Every chapter should advance at least 2 threads. The main plot thread should
advance in every chapter. Secondary threads can go quiet for 1–2 chapters but
must resurface before readers forget them.

Good thread rhythm for a 10-chapter story:
- Chapters 1–3:   Establish all threads. Main plot dominates.
- Chapters 4–5:   Secondary threads deepen. Main plot complicates.
- Chapter 6:      Emotional/relationship thread peaks. Main plot pauses briefly.
- Chapters 7–8:   Main plot accelerates. All threads converge.
- Chapter 9:      All threads at maximum tension simultaneously.
- Chapter 10:     All threads resolve — main plot first, emotional last.

________________________________________________________________________________

ACTION BEAT GUIDELINES
For genres that include physical conflict, chase, or confrontation.

Action beats work best when they are:
- Grounded — in real physical space with real consequences
- Short — 300–600 words maximum per beat
- Purposeful — they must change the situation or reveal character
- Varied — use different types across chapters (a chase, a confrontation, an
  attack, a standoff)
- Costly — someone should be hurt, cornered, or exposed afterward

Place action beats at these structural moments:
- End of Act 1 (around chapter 25%) — stakes become real
- Mid-Act 2 (around chapter 50%) — darkest moment escalation
- Late Act 2 (around chapter 75%) — everything closing in
- Act 3 climax — the final confrontation or escape

Avoid: action in consecutive chapters (it numbs the reader), action that
resolves cleanly (it should cost something), action described from too far away
(keep the camera close).

________________________________________________________________________________

THE SLOW-BURN ROMANCE ARC
For any story with a romantic secondary thread. Use this 7-beat progression
regardless of chapter count.

| Beat                     | What Happens                                    | Never Say It Directly |
|--------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|------------------------|
| 1. Friction              | They meet as adversaries or strangers           | The reader should feel the charge before the characters do |
| 2. Reluctant usefulness  | They need each other and resent it              | First cooperation. No warmth yet. |
| 3. Accidental glimpse    | One sees the other unguarded                    | A moment of vulnerability, quickly covered |
| 4. First trust           | One confides something true                     | Small. Not a declaration. A crack in the wall. |
| 5. The choice            | One protects the other at personal cost         | This is the turn. The reader knows before the characters do. |
| 6. The almost            | A moment interrupted or withdrawn               | Should be the most frustrating chapter in the book |
| 7. The admission         | Something honest is said                        | Understate it. Let the reader do the work. |

SESSION WORKFLOW
Follow this every time you open your LLM to write.

Step 1 — Paste the Master System Prompt
Step 2 — Paste the Chapter Prompt you're working on
Step 3 — Request Scene 1 only (not the whole chapter)
Step 4 — Review, adjust, use Continuation or Reset prompts if needed
Step 5 — Request Scene 2
Step 6 — Repeat for Scene 3
Step 7 — Request the cliffhanger separately if the LLM didn't nail it
Step 8 — Save your chapter output before closing the session
Step 9 — At the start of the next session, briefly summarize the previous
         chapter in 2–3 sentences and paste it before the System Prompt as a
         "Previously:" block

The "Previously:" block is important. Local LLMs have no memory between
sessions. A 2–3 sentence recap of the previous chapter keeps the narrative
continuous and prevents the LLM from contradicting events that already happened.

GENRE-SPECIFIC REMINDERS

Mystery / Thriller
- Plant all clues before chapter 75% — no new information in the finale
- The antagonist must appear in Act 1, even if not identified as antagonist
- Every red herring needs a logical explanation once cleared

Romance
- The central relationship must face at least one external obstacle and one
  internal obstacle
- The lowest point (almost-separation) should occur around chapter 80%
- Resolution must be earned — no sudden personality changes

Fantasy / Sci-Fi
- Establish your world's rules in Act 1 and never break them in Act 3
- Magic / technology solves problems only if introduced before the problem
  appears
- One new world-building element per chapter maximum

Historical Fiction
- Ground at least one detail per scene in documented historical fact
- Characters can be fictional; events they witness should not contradict history
- Research before writing, not after — anachronisms break the spell

Horror
- Threat should escalate in specificity, not just intensity
- Safety must feel possible for fear to work — if everything is always
  dangerous, nothing is scary
- The worst thing usually happens slightly before the end, not at it

Adventure
- Each act should change the physical location significantly
- The protagonist must fail meaningfully before the final success
- Companions can be lost — lone arrival at the goal is more powerful than a
  group finish
